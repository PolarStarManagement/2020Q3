Section 3 Calendar Spreads

3.1 Nomenclature and Example

In this section we show the big curve moves across a universe of commodities. For the purpose of this analysis we mainly focus on the front ends of the respective commodity futures curves. As a rule of thumb we look at the first contract after the front month and three months out from that point. We suppose short exposure in the spreads, i.e short the contract nearest expiry and long the one furthest from expiry.

The plot below shows the value of $1 invested by shorting the spreads of the four commodities shown. Recall the super-contango that was present in the crude oil market following the corona virus crisis. During this period the nearer dated crude oil contracts traded at extreme discounts relative to the further dated contracts. In such a case, shorting the spreads will be a very profitable strategy as seen in the facet plot below. Notice the sharp correction that followed. This happened after the OPEC+ nations decided to curtail the production of crude oil following the severe damand destruction of the corona virus crisis.

3.2 Calendar Spread Changes

The plot below shows the curve moves across a collection of commodities during the third quarter of 2020. Green and red bars represent increases and decreases in spreads respectively. The vertical dashed line shows the mean absolute percentage change across all the commodities shown.

Figure 3.2: 2020 Q3 short calendar price changes expressed as percentages

The imbalance of green versus red bars shows that the majority of commodity curves moved in a more backwardated direction during the third quarter. Shorting the WTI crude oil curve would only have yielded a 1.81% return. Shorting the Lumber, Lean Hog and Cheese spreads (pun intended) would have resulted in large losses of 22.45%, 17.61% and 11.56% respectively.
